# Sprint-Review-and-RetroSpective
A document + slideshow that reviews Scrum and Agile methods used during a project


How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this?

  If you have the luxury of sitting down face to face with a client or user, you could begin by asking them questions about what features they would like to see implemented.
  I imagine many people aren't in the position or in need of doing that, so you must instead put yourself in the shoes of the user and craft the user stories yourself
  based on what you know of the project's goals. Interepreting a users needs is part listening, part note taking.
  User Stories can be recorded a number of ways but essentially they are a statement about a feature or thing they want, 
  which in turn gives you the work of how to fufill that want or need. User stories are just tools to help break down work into manageable chunks and keep perspective on the
  work being done. 

How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?

  If I were to be starting from scratch, I would begin by defining what is the least amount I need to do in order to get a working product. The smaller and leaner the program
  could be, the better. Focusing on modularity, and essential core systems that won't change much later on. If I am working on another project thats been in development for some time,
  I would communicate with the team to get caught up on what needs to be done. Instead of trying to read every line of each class, I would focus on just solving the problems 
  that are required of me. Small changes, testing often, just making sure I wasn't breaking another part of the system. Agile can help with the communcation on a team, as well as 
  helping manage time. With sprints and other daily rituals, you keep moving forward and stay on a rhythmn.

What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?

Being a good communicator, being honest in your assessments, being humble, and being open to learning new things all the time. Just from the small experience I have coding, 
I can tell that working on a large team would require many skills outside of the coding itself. Programming is a very complex topic, and nobody knows it all, so when its time
to listen and learn, just showing that level of effort could make you much more effective and well liked by your peers.
